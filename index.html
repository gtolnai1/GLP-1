GLP-1 Agonists, Metabolic Health, & Body Composition

Required Reading

Incretin-based therapies, such as GLP-1 receptor agonists (e.g., semaglutide), are medications that mimic hormones naturally released by L-cells in the gut. Biologically, these hormones regulate energy balance by increasing satiety, slowing gastric emptying, and suppressing appetite in the brain.

While highly effective for weight loss, popular health communicators (like the "Glucose Goddess" blog) have raised concerns about the loss of lean mass (muscle and water), which is vital for maintaining a healthy basal metabolic rate and physical strength as we age.

A 2026 systematic review and meta-analysis of 20 Randomised Controlled Trials (RCTs) involving over 15,000 participants provided formal clinical data on this. The study found that 25–39% of weight lost on incretin therapies is indeed lean mass. However, the analysis clarified that this proportional loss is broadly comparable to standard lifestyle interventions (26.2%). Most importantly, combining lifestyle changes with resistance training demonstrated the most favorable profile, dropping lean mass loss to just 17.5%. Accurate DXA scans or MRIs are frequently used in these studies to properly monitor the changing ratio of fat to functional muscle.

Glossary of Terms

Adipose Tissue (Fat Mass): Stored lipid tissue used by the body as an energy reserve.

Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR): The minimum amount of energy the body requires at rest to maintain essential physiological functions; it is supported and increased by having a higher muscle mass.

Dual-Energy X-ray Absorptiometry (DXA): A highly precise medical imaging scan used in clinical studies to accurately monitor body composition and distinguish between fat mass and lean mass.

Gastric Emptying: The process by which food leaves the stomach and enters the small intestine; GLP-1 hormones act to slow this speed of digestion down.

GLP-1 (Glucagon-Like Peptide-1): A peptide hormone produced primarily by the L-cells in the intestines in response to food intake. Its main functions are to stimulate insulin secretion, inhibit glucagon release, slow down gastric emptying, and promote a feeling of fullness (satiety).

GLP-1 Receptor Agonists: A class of incretin-based medications (such as semaglutide and liraglutide) that mimic the body's natural GLP-1 gut hormones to regulate energy balance.

Homeostatic Regulation: The physiological processes the body uses to maintain a stable internal environment, which includes the regulation of hunger and energy balance by gut hormones.

Incretins: Hormones released by the gut (such as GLP-1) that play a major role in digestion, insulin secretion, and homeostatic hunger regulation.

L-Cells (Large Granule Cells): Specialized enteroendocrine cells located in the gut that release hormones to help the body feel full, slow down digestion, and reduce appetite. The "L" stands for "Large," named by early histologists because these cells contain large, dense-core secretory granules (which store the hormones) when viewed under an electron microscope.

Lean Mass: The body's total weight excluding fat, which primarily consists of water and functional muscle tissue.

Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI): A medical imaging technique utilized in scientific studies as a highly accurate tool for monitoring specific changes in body composition.

Meta-Analysis: A robust statistical technique that combines and analyzes data from multiple independent studies to reach a more definitive conclusion than a single study could provide.

Random-Effects Models: A specific statistical method used in meta-analyses to pool data while accounting for the natural variability and differences between the various studies.

Randomised Controlled Trials (RCTs): An experimental design considered the "gold standard" for evaluating medical interventions, where participants are randomly assigned to either a treatment group or a control group to prevent bias.

Resistance Training: Physical exercise focused on building and maintaining muscle mass, which is shown to significantly preserve lean mass during periods of significant weight reduction.

Satiety: The biological feeling of fullness and satisfaction after eating, which is increased by the signaling of incretin hormones.
